A Candid Turkey Wrap and the Framework I Used to Make It

Home Page

Description

Today, I logged into The Odin Project to complete my final recipe page for my HTML coding project and could't be bothered to come up with anything. Instead, I decided to make lunch normally and write down whatever steps I used to make it. That's why you get a boring turkey wrap today, but it actually turned out to be one of my favorite meals out of all of these! I won't spoil the details here as the process is the highlight of this page moreso than the recipe itself.

Main Ingredients

  • There are none! Look in your fridge and start with that!

Ingredients Used

  • Braised whole turkey chunks
  • Lettuce
  • Grape tomatoes
  • Chick-fil-A Ranch
  • Pickled onions
  • Extra virgin olive oil
  • Various herbs

Steps

  1. Select a Starting Ingredient

    The first step is to identify a starting point. More often than not, this will be a protein or other ingredient that's about to go bad. For me, it was a braised whole turkey that checked all the boxes. Not only did I have way too much of it but I'm not the biggest fan of the stuff and knew I wouldn't eat much of it if left in that plain state.

  2. Brainstorm a Flavor Profile

    I tried to think of what might taste good with turkey. This step can sometimes feel a bit overwhelming, but the simplest place to find inspiration is to think of recipes that prominently feature the ingredient. The first thing that came to my mind was a chicken bacon ranch profile since turkey is similar, and it's a common enough pairing.

  3. Use What You Have

    When I checked, I realized that I was out of bacon and had to scrap the idea. If you're stumped, you can always look at what you have to see if any of it would help round out your starting ingredient. Really, I was craving something fresh and bright, and it finally clicked: I was going to make a cold cut style wrap. Mike's way. I knew I had some salad vegetables in the fridge, a few sauces/seasonings, and tortillas, so I went with it.

  4. Balance the Flavors and Textures

    I had three objectives to turn my turkey into something I could enjoy:

    1. Deal with the dryness that often accompanies white meat
    2. Use a strong flavor to cover up the flavors of turkey that I don't like
    3. Add texture contrast
    You may use plain mayo, but I started with a layer of Chick-fil-A ranch because I find it has a lighter, tangier flavor compared other brands. A sauce aids in all three of my objectives. Next, I sprinkled in some dried herbs and drizzled some extra virgin olive oil for a little bit of subtle aromatics. I believe I used parsley, thyme, oregano, and basil, but you can just sniff and add whatever herbs seem to go with yours. Then I added a base of lettuce that was starting to look a bit off. Letuce brightens the flavor and textural body in a way that other ingredients can't! From here on, just assume I cut everything relatively thin, so that no one ingredient takes over an entire bite. I then added my turkey and layered on salted tomato slices to add juicy, sweet, sour, salty, and umami flavor all in one ingredient. Tomato is a powerhouse but usually needs something like lettuce to balance it's wet texture. Finally, I threw on some pickled red onions I happened to have made earlier. Fellas. I freaking love pickled onions because, as with any pickle, they add crucial sourness, juiciness, and crispness that makes any dish better. This is my key strong ingredient that can really change the final dish. If you've never made pickled onions, trust me when I say the crispness is unlike any other pickle.

  5. Serve

    When it's ready, all you have to do is attempt to roll it up before realizing you have severely over-loaded it and resigning to eat it street taco style. I just thought calling it "candid taco" would be a bit misleading. I'm sorry for not properly describing the manner in which I enclosed the filling in tortilla I will commit seppuku now.
